Glossary_Page_1_Title_1 = TEU
Glossary_Page_1_Text_1 = “Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit“ is the standard unit for a container. By the way, a standard container is 20 ft long, 8 ft high and 8 ft wide.
Glossary_Page_1_Title_2 = dwt
Glossary_Page_1_Text_2 = “Deadweight tonnage“, this is the weight in tons that a container ship can carry.
Glossary_Page_1_Title_3 = kn
Glossary_Page_1_Text_3 = This stands for “knots”, the unit of speed on water. 1 kn=1.151 mph.
Glossary_Page_1_Title_4 = Feeder
Glossary_Page_1_Text_4 = The smallest container ships are called Feeders, because they “feed” the larger ports. Smaller ports can only be accessed by Feeders.
Glossary_Page_1_Title_5 = Panamax
Glossary_Page_1_Text_5 = Panamax ships are larger than Feeders and are called so because they conform to the pre-2014 Panama Canal sizes, and thus are able to travel through it.
Glossary_Page_1_Title_6 = Post-Panamax
Glossary_Page_1_Text_6 = Post-Panamax ships are the next largest class after Panamax. They exceed the size of the old Panama Canal, which was actually enlarged in 2014.
Glossary_Page_1_Title_7 = New-Panamax
Glossary_Page_1_Text_7 = The New Panamax ships exceed the Post-Panamax ships in both length and width. They are designed based on the dimensions of the new Panama Canal, which was expanded in 2014, and thus are able to travel through it.
Glossary_Page_1_Title_8 = ULCV
Glossary_Page_1_Text_8 = “Ultra Large Container Vessels“ are so big that they are unable to travel through the Panama Canal. They can transport up to 18,000 containers.
